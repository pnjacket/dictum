# Concern 11.11 — Observability & Monitoring

> **One-line:** What the running system reveals about itself — logs, metrics, traces, SLOs, alerts — and the audit trail that realizes Governance's audit policy.

## Purpose & Scope

Active when the product is a running service. Owns **telemetry** and the **audit-trail mechanism**. It does **not** own performance *targets* (Performance & Scalability) nor audit *policy* (Governance) — it implements/measures both.

## Sub-aspects (the scope vocabulary)

| Sub-aspect | key | Default (when deployed) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Per-`COMPONENT` telemetry | `per-component-telemetry` | in | Logs / metrics / traces emitted |
| SLIs / SLOs | `slis-slos` | in when targets exist | Measure Performance's targets |
| Alerting rules | `alerting-rules` | in for production | Each rule guards an `SLO-###` and routes to a role + runbook |
| Audit-trail mechanism | `audit-trail-mechanism` | in when audit required | Realizes Governance `POLICY` |
| Dashboards | `dashboards` | optional | |
| Log/audit retention | `log-audit-retention` | in when regulated | Per Governance policy |

## Owned Contracts (referenced by stable ID)

1. **Per-`COMPONENT` telemetry spec** — logs/metrics/traces each `COMPONENT-###` emits.
2. **SLO/SLI definitions + alert rules** — `SLO-###`, measuring Performance's targets, and `ALERT-###` (a local prefix) for the rules that guard them. **Referenced by** Operations (runbooks/alerts).
3. **Audit-trail mechanism** — realizes Governance's audit `POLICY-###` (structured, tamper-evident where required) with stated retention.

## Consumed (referenced, not owned)

- `COMPONENT-###` (Architecture); audit `POLICY-###` (Governance); performance targets (Performance & Scalability).

## Maturity Ladder — what each rung means HERE

| Rung | Meaning for Observability & Monitoring |
|---|---|
| **Absent** | Not deployed / no telemetry. |
| **Sketch** | What's logged/monitored, as bullets. |
| **Specified** | Telemetry approach per signal; alerts noted. |
| **Contract-grade** | Per-`COMPONENT-###` signals + `SLO-###`/SLIs + alert rules + audit trail per Governance policy + retention. An operator can observe, alert, and audit without guessing. |
| **Verified** | *(delivery methodology)* Telemetry emitting, alerts firing, audit captured. |

## Contract-grade bar

Per-component signals + SLOs/SLIs + alert rules + audit trail per Governance policy + retention.

- **An alert rule is a complete routing row, not a bullet.** When `alerting-rules` is in scope, each rule states: the `SLO-###` it guards, its trigger (threshold + evaluation window, or an error-budget burn rate), its severity, a **`ROLE-###` notification target — a role, never a person** — and its `RUNBOOK-###` response reference. That is what "an operator can alert without guessing" cashes out to: the row is wireable into any alerting backend as written.

## Worked Example (a planning SaaS)

`11-monitoring.md` + the audit *mechanism* in `10-audit-logging.md` (the `audit-service`) are this concern. At *Contract-grade*: each service emits health + key metrics; Kong access logs; `SLO-VIEW-OPEN` derived from the Performance target for view-open latency; the `audit-service` implements the trail satisfying Governance's `POLICY` for auditable model/security events, retained per policy. (`alerting-rules` scoped out pre-production — recorded in *Non-goals*.)

## Worked Example (a realtime collaboration service — `alerting-rules` in scope)

The planning SaaS sits at the single-SLO end. A multi-tenant realtime collaboration service (shared-document co-editing over a fan-out event surface) shows the other end at *Contract-grade* — multiple SLOs, each measuring a Performance (11.13) target, with authored alert rules.

**SLO register:**

| SLO | Measures | SLI | Objective + window |
|---|---|---|---|
| `SLO-EDIT-DELIVERY` | `PERF-EDIT-DELIVERY` | p95 edit-propagation latency, publisher→other participants, end-to-end | ≤ 250 ms, 30-day rolling |
| `SLO-JOIN-AVAILABILITY` | `PERF-JOIN-AVAILABILITY` | successful session joins / attempted joins | ≥ 99.9%, calendar month |
| `SLO-FANOUT-LAG` | `PERF-FANOUT-LAG` | p99 cross-replica fan-out lag at the broker seam | ≤ 2 s, 30-day rolling |

**Alert register** (every row carries the full contract fields — SLO guarded, trigger, severity, role, runbook):

| Alert | Guards | Trigger | Severity | Notify | Response |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| `ALERT-EDIT-DELIVERY-FASTBURN` | `SLO-EDIT-DELIVERY` | error-budget burn rate ≥ 14.4× over a 1 h window (≈ 2% of the 30-day budget in 1 h) | page | `ROLE-ONCALL-OPERATOR` | `RUNBOOK-DELIVERY-DEGRADED` |
| `ALERT-FANOUT-LAG-SUSTAINED` | `SLO-FANOUT-LAG` | p99 lag > 2 s sustained for 10 min | ticket | `ROLE-ONCALL-OPERATOR` | `RUNBOOK-FANOUT-BACKPRESSURE` |

`ROLE-ONCALL-OPERATOR` is Security's (11.7) role register — a role, never a person, so the rule survives staff turnover; `RUNBOOK-###` responses are Operations' (11.10). An unguarded SLO (no alert row) is a deliberate scope-out, recorded in *Non-goals*, not an omission.

## Design Decisions

| # | Decision | Rationale |
|---|----------|-----------|
| 1 | Owns the audit-trail *mechanism*; Governance owns the *policy*. | Same facts/policy/mechanism split as data protection; audit implementation lives with the rest of telemetry. |
| 2 | Telemetry is specified per `COMPONENT-###`. | Ties what's observable to the architecture, so no component is a black box. |
| 3 | Observability owns SLOs/alerts; Performance owns the targets. | A clean targets→SLI/SLO→alert chain keeps perf budgets with Performance and their measurement with telemetry. |
| 4 | Contract-grade requires alerts + audit, not just logs. | Logs alone don't tell you when something's wrong or prove what happened; alerts and the audit trail are what make a service operable and accountable. |
| 5 | An alert rule is authored as a complete routing row: guarded `SLO-###`, trigger (threshold + window or burn rate), severity, `ROLE-###` target, `RUNBOOK-###` response. | "Alert on high latency" as a bullet leaves the paging target and the response to be guessed during the incident; naming the fields makes the row wireable into any backend, and role-not-person targets keep the rule valid across staff turnover. |
